Multiphysics Simulations of Spark Plasma Sintering Process

A Two-Stage Approach for Material Parameters Identification in Spark Plasma Sintering

Project Overview

This project presents a numerical–experimental framework to identify key material parameters in spark plasma sintering (SPS). By dividing the densification process into two stages—initial (high porosity) and final (low porosity); a more accurate determination of porosity-dependent parameters was achieved. The method combines COMSOL Multiphysics simulations with experimental validation on copper and nickel powders, enabling reliable predictions across different heating rates.

Problem & Motivation

Accurate SPS modeling requires knowledge of porosity-dependent material parameters such as shear and bulk viscous moduli. Existing approaches often rely on oversimplified homogenization assumptions or expensive, specialized experiments. These limitations lead to poor prediction of density evolution across varying heating rates, which is critical for optimizing SPS processes.

Key Contributions

  • Proposed a two-stage framework for parameter identification, separating high-porosity and low-porosity regimes.
  • Achieved average prediction error below 1.5% for copper and nickel SPS experiments.
  • Validated the model for heating rates of 25, 50, and 100 K/min, ensuring robustness.
  • Reduced computational effort by restricting inverse parameter identification to the initial stage only.
  • Demonstrated transferability of the method to multiple materials.

Technical Highlights

  • Tools & Platforms: COMSOL Multiphysics (FEM-based SPS model), experimental SPS setup.
  • Models Used: Olevsky’s continuum sintering model, Norton-Green creep law, composite sphere homogenization.
  • Methods: Coupled electro-thermal-mechanical FEM, inverse parameter identification, experimental validation with copper & nickel powders.
  • Parameters Identified: Porosity-dependent shear modulus (φ) and bulk modulus (ψ).

Results & Impact

The framework delivered highly accurate predictions of density and temperature evolution across different heating rates, significantly outperforming conventional homogenization-based methods. It provides a systematic and generalizable approach to parameter identification, reducing experimental costs and improving the reliability of SPS simulations. This work advances the ability to optimize porous material processing, with potential applications across metals and ceramics.
