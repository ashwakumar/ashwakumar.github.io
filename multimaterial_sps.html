Multi-Material SPS with Direct FE2 Framework

Predicting Interface Behavior and Final Geometry in Multi-Material Spark Plasma Sintering

  • Main Author: Ashwani Kumar
  • Affiliation: ETH Zurich
  • Online: SSRN
  • Co-Authors: M. Bernet, L. Deillon, M. Bambach, M. Afrasiabi
  • Date: 3rd Nov 2025
  • Journal: SSRN (Preprint)

Project Overview

Multi-material spark plasma sintering (SPS) enables architected components with tailored properties but introduces challenges in controlling differential densification, geometric precision, and interface alignment. This project develops and uniquely validates a fully coupled electro–thermo–mechanical model for Cu-Ni systems, enabling precise prediction of final geometries and interface behavior.

Problem & Motivation

Experimental trial-and-error in multi-material SPS offers limited insight into the complex electro-thermo-mechanical conditions that govern sintering. Challenges such as porosity imbalance driving interface displacement and localized Joule heating effects make it difficult to achieve precise geometric tolerances and bonding quality without predictive tools.

Key Contributions

  • Developed a fully coupled electro–thermo–mechanical finite element model for multi-material SPS.
  • Validated temperature histories and densification kinetics across three interface orientations (horizontal, inclined, vertical) with <1% deviation.
  • Achieved >93% accuracy in predicting final geometries and interface displacements.
  • Identified that interface orientation influences current paths more strongly than applied thermal loads.
  • Proposed initial interface location as a key design lever for alignment control.

Technical Highlights

  • Tools & Platforms: Finite Element Analysis (FEA), Multiphysics Simulation.
  • Methods: Porosity-dependent constitutive parameter calibration, transferability from single to multi-material systems without extra fitting.
  • Validation: Comparison with experimental trials on Copper-Nickel (Cu-Ni) systems.

Results & Impact

The simulations uncovered that interface orientation critically governs densification behavior by altering current paths and localized heating. The validated framework serves as a powerful design tool, enabling engineers to optimize powder deposition strategies and predict component shape before manufacturing. This reduces experimental waste and accelerates the development of complex multi-material components.
