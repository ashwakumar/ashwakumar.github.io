About me

I am Ashwani Kumar, a computational engineering researcher specializing in multiphysics and multiscale modeling. Currently pursuing my Doctor of Science in Mechanical Engineering at ETH Zürich, I focus on advanced simulation techniques for manufacturing processes such as Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS). My work combines finite element methods, machine learning, and material modeling to bridge microscale phenomena with macroscale engineering applications.

With professional experience spanning academia and industry—from developing fracture models in complex materials to leading structural analysis in the automotive sector—I bring both technical depth and practical problem-solving skills. I am passionate about applying computational tools to innovate in manufacturing, materials science, and product design.

Outside of work, I enjoy exploring new technologies and collaborating on multidisciplinary projects that push the boundaries of engineering research.

Portfolio

  • Multiscale Modeling of SPS with Direct FE2 Framework

    Multiscale

    Developed a novel Direct FE2 framework that integrates microscale powder characteristics with macroscale electro-thermal-mechanical effects, improving both accuracy and computational efficiency for SPS simulations.

  • Multi-Material SPS Interface Behavior

    Multiphysics

    Developed a validated fully coupled electro-thermo-mechanical framework for multi-material SPS, predicting interface behavior and final geometry with high accuracy.

  • Multiphysics Simulations of Spark Plasma Sintering Process

    Multiphysics

    Developed a two-stage numerical–experimental approach to identify material parameters in spark plasma sintering, enabling accurate density prediction across different heating rates for copper and nickel. This method improves accuracy, reduces experimental cost, and is transferable to other SPS materials.

  • Bone Tunnel Stress in AC Joint Reconstruction

    Web design

    Studied how bone tunnel placement in coracoclavicular ligament reconstruction influences shear stresses in the coracoid process. Using finite element modeling and radiological analysis of patient data, the project identified safer tunnel configurations that minimize fracture risk and improve surgical outcomes.

  • Stress Regime Effects on Carbonate Permeability

    Web development

    Investigated the impact of changing tectonic stress regimes on fracture aperture and permeability in the Latemar carbonate buildup (Dolomites). Through outcrop data, finite element geomechanics, and flow simulations, the study showed that superimposed stresses increase permeability by up to 62%, highlighting the importance of stress history in predicting fluid flow in fractured reservoirs.
